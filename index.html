Movement I — Age is climbing

The middle thinned. The top thickened.

Split into three broad bands, the mix barbelled. The 31–50 core lost the most ground, while the 51+ band grew its share the most — and held on more tightly than anyone younger.

The oldest band held on hardest: 64% of the 51+ group stayed, versus 38% of the 31–50 core. A 51+ employee was 1.7× more likely to keep their seat than someone in mid-career.
Movement II — Tenure is deepening

Newest hires first. Veterans stayed.

By years of service, the shape is a clean staircase. The under-one-year band collapsed from 25 people to 6, while everything from five years up came through nearly whole — pulling median tenure from 2.7 to 4.8 years.

Retention rises with every step of service — 20% → 35% → 67% → 82% → 100%. Every one of the six employees with a decade or more stayed. The median departing employee had just 1.4 years in.
Together — one core, two readings

Older and more tenured are the same story.

These two movements aren't competing — they're the same people seen twice. The long-service employees the transformation kept are also, on the whole, the older ones. Retaining the veterans is what lifted both the tenure line and the age line at once.

For planning, the pair points one direction: the company is now built around a smaller, more senior core — real stability and institutional knowledge today, and a succession and knowledge-transfer question to watch as that core ages.
